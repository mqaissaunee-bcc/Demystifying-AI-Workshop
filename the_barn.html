About the Book

The Barn: The Secret History of a Murder in Mississippi (2024) is a work of narrative nonfiction — a genre that uses the techniques of storytelling (scene-setting, character development, narrative tension) to report true events. Wright Thompson investigates the 1955 murder of Emmett Till, a 14-year-old Black boy from Chicago who was abducted, tortured, and killed while visiting relatives in the Mississippi Delta.

Thompson reveals new details about where the crime took place — a barn outside Drew, Mississippi — and who was involved. He situates the murder within a vast web of history: slavery, sharecropping, the cotton economy, white supremacy, and the systematic suppression of truth that followed the crime. This is not just a book about one event; it is a book about how history works, and how silence shapes what we know.

The book was named one of TIME's 100 Must-Read Books of 2024 and received starred reviews from Kirkus and Booklist.

About the Author

Wright Thompson is a senior writer at ESPN and a long-form journalist known for deeply reported, literary profiles. He grew up in the Mississippi Delta — just 23 miles from the barn where Emmett Till was murdered. That proximity, and the fact that he didn't learn the details of the crime until he left the state for college, is itself one of the book's central arguments: silence about racial violence is deliberate and regional.

His earlier books include The Cost of These Dreams (collected journalism) and Pappyland (about the bourbon industry and family). The Barn is his most ambitious and personal work to date.

Genre: Narrative Nonfiction

Narrative nonfiction (sometimes called "literary journalism" or "creative nonfiction") reports true events using storytelling techniques. Authors like Thompson use scene-setting, dialogue, character portraits, and a narrative arc — but every detail must be documented and accurate.

As you read, notice: How does Thompson build suspense? Whose perspective do we follow? When does he insert his own voice? These are craft choices with purpose — they affect what we understand and how we feel about what we learn.

Historical Context: What You Need to Know

Jim Crow Laws (1877–1965)

"Jim Crow" refers to state and local laws across the American South that legally enforced racial segregation — separating Black and white Americans in schools, transportation, restaurants, voting booths, and everyday life. These laws enforced a racial hierarchy backed by law and by violence.

The Mississippi Delta

The Delta is a flat, fertile region in northwestern Mississippi shaped by centuries of cotton farming. The wealth of that region was built on enslaved labor, and after the Civil War, on sharecropping — a system that kept Black families trapped in cycles of debt and economic dependence on white landowners. By 1955, the Delta was a place of extreme racial inequality.

Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

One year before Till's murder, the Supreme Court ruled that segregated public schools were unconstitutional. White supremacists in the South saw this ruling as an existential threat — and racial terror increased in the aftermath. Till's murder was part of this backlash.

The Great Migration

Between roughly 1910 and 1970, millions of Black Americans left the South for Northern and Midwestern cities — escaping racial violence and seeking economic opportunity. Emmett Till's family had made this journey from Mississippi to Chicago. His murder happened during a summer visit "down South."

Major Themes at a Glance

  • Silence and Suppression: The cover-up of Till's murder began immediately and continues in some ways today. Thompson argues that silence about racial violence is not passive — it is a choice made by individuals, institutions, and communities.
  • Power, Land, and Property: Thompson traces how control of land in the Delta determined who had power, who was vulnerable, and what crimes could be committed without consequence.
  • Memory and Justice: What does justice look like decades after a crime? Who gets to decide what is remembered, and what is forgotten?
  • Individual Courage: The book honors witnesses like Willie Reed and activists like Mamie Till-Mobley who risked their safety to tell the truth.
  • The Author's Positionality: Thompson is a white man from Mississippi writing about anti-Black violence. He is transparent about what this means — what he could see, and what he couldn't.

Close Reading & Analysis

Close reading means paying careful attention to how a text is written, not just what it says. In this section, you'll practice reading strategies and analyze the craft and argument of The Barn.

What Is Thompson Arguing?

A key skill in reading nonfiction is identifying the author's central argument — the main claim they are making and the evidence they use to support it. Thompson's central argument is not simply that "the murder of Emmett Till was horrific." That is a fact. His argument is more specific:

The full truth of Till's murder — where it happened, who was involved, and why it was covered up — has been deliberately suppressed. Understanding that suppression is essential to understanding America.

As you read, track: What evidence does Thompson provide? What sources does he use (court records, oral histories, family accounts, geography)? Where does he acknowledge uncertainty? Where does he make interpretive leaps, and does he signal them clearly?

Key Literary and Journalistic Concepts

Point of View

Thompson writes in third person for most of the historical narrative, but also inserts his own first-person perspective — his memories, his shame, his family's connection to the Delta. Ask yourself: Why does he do this? What does the author's presence add to, or complicate, the story?

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

A primary source is firsthand evidence: a court transcript, an interview with a witness, a photograph taken at the time. A secondary source interprets primary sources: a historian's analysis, a news article written years later. Thompson draws on both. Notice how he signals which kind of evidence he's using.

Narrative Structure

The book does not move in simple chronological order. Thompson weaves between deep history (medieval Europe, Indigenous Mississippi, the slave trade), the events of 1955, and the present-day effort to memorialize the barn. This non-linear structure makes an argument: the past is not over. It lives inside the present.

The "Blast Radius" Metaphor

Thompson uses the phrase blast radius of shame to describe how the murder's consequences spread outward — through families, communities, generations, and the landscape itself. A metaphor is a comparison that illuminates an idea. What does "blast radius" suggest about the nature and scale of the crime's effects?

Concept Map: Forces Behind the Crime

Thompson argues that Till's murder was not the act of a few "bad men" — it was made possible by interlocking historical forces. Click each node to learn more.

→ →
Cotton Economy: The Delta's entire social order was built on cotton farming — first through enslaved labor, then sharecropping. Control of land and cotton meant control of people. Thompson traces this back centuries to show how deeply economic exploitation was woven into the region.
Sharecropping System: After slavery ended, sharecropping kept Black families legally "free" but economically bound. Farmers worked white-owned land in exchange for a "share" of the crop — but the accounting was controlled by the landowner, perpetuating debt and dependency. The Milam and Bryant families were poor white sharecroppers who enforced racial hierarchy partly because it was one of the few ways they had status.
White Economic Anxiety: By 1955, the cotton economy was failing — machines were replacing labor. The Milams and Bryants were economically desperate. Thompson argues that racial violence often intensifies when poor white men fear losing what little power they have. Emmett Till — a confident Black teenager from Chicago — threatened the racial hierarchy they depended on psychologically.
→ →
Jim Crow Laws: Legal segregation meant Black Mississippians could not serve on juries, could rarely testify against white men, and faced terror if they challenged the racial order. The law did not protect them — it protected their killers.
Guaranteed Impunity: Roy Bryant and J.W. Milam were acquitted by an all-white jury in just over an hour. They later gave a partial "confession" to a journalist — knowing they could not be tried again for the same crime (double jeopardy). The legal system's failure to punish them was not a malfunction; it was the system working as designed for that time and place.
Community Silence: Witnesses like Willie Reed left Mississippi out of fear. Local newspapers and officials downplayed or buried the story. White communities chose not to name what happened — because doing so would have required confronting what their community had done and permitted. Thompson's book names what silence protected.

A Note on This Kind of Reading

Reading books about racial violence can bring up strong emotions — discomfort, anger, grief, or even resistance. These responses are part of critical reading, not obstacles to it. Notice what you feel as you read, and ask yourself: Why am I feeling this? What in the text produced this response? What does my reaction tell me about what I already believed?

You are not required to have experienced racism to engage with this book thoughtfully — but you are invited to be honest about where you're starting from.

Thompson begins his history of the Mississippi Delta with the Walford Site — an Indigenous agricultural community near the spot where Till would later be murdered. He draws parallels between that early monoculture economy (controlling land and labor for a single crop) and the cotton plantation system that followed. The Delta has been shaped by exploitation of land and people for over a thousand years.

After the Civil War, the 13th Amendment abolished slavery — but new systems quickly replaced it. Sharecropping, convict leasing, and Jim Crow laws kept Black Southerners economically and legally subordinated. In the Mississippi Delta, the same families that had owned enslaved people continued to control land, labor, and local politics. Freedom was nominal; the power structures of slavery persisted in new forms.

Over six million Black Americans left the South for Northern cities during this period — escaping racial terror, poverty, and legal discrimination in search of jobs and safety. Emmett Till's mother, Mamie Till-Mobley, was part of this migration from Mississippi to Chicago. Emmett grew up in Chicago — which is why he was unfamiliar with the deadly racial codes of the Mississippi Delta when he visited in 1955.

The Supreme Court unanimously ruled that racial segregation in public schools was unconstitutional. For Black Americans, this was a landmark victory. For white supremacists in the South, it was a threat — proof that the racial order could be legally challenged and dismantled. The backlash was swift and violent. Thompson frames Till's murder as part of this post-Brown terror campaign.

Roy Bryant and J.W. Milam, half-brothers, abducted 14-year-old Emmett Till from his great-uncle Moses Wright's home in the middle of the night. They took him to a barn outside Drew, Mississippi, where — Thompson reveals — at least eight people participated in a four-hour torture and murder. Till's body was dumped in the Tallahatchie River with a cotton gin fan tied around his neck. A fisherman discovered the body three days later.

❓ The book reveals there were more perpetrators than the two men charged. What does that tell you about how this crime was committed — and how it was covered up?

Bryant and Milam were tried for murder. An all-white, all-male jury deliberated for just 67 minutes before acquitting them. Moses Wright, Till's great-uncle, was one of the few Black witnesses who testified — a courageous act in that environment. After the trial, protected by double jeopardy (they could not be retried), Bryant and Milam gave a partial confession to journalist William Bradford Huie — but it was misleading about where the murder took place.

Glossary & Key Figures

Use this section to look up key terms and people as you read. Each entry includes a plain-language definition and a note on why it matters for understanding the book.

Key Terms & Concepts

Narrative Nonfiction
True writing that uses storytelling techniques — scenes, characters, tension, voice — to report real events. Every fact must be documented, but the writing reads like a story.
Why it matters: Understanding this genre helps you see how Thompson's choices — what to describe, whose voice to center — are arguments, not just decoration.
Jim Crow Laws
State and local laws (roughly 1877–1965) that legally enforced racial segregation in the American South, requiring separate facilities for Black and white people and limiting Black voting, education, and legal rights.
Why it matters: Jim Crow created the legal environment that made Till's murder possible and his killers' acquittal inevitable.
Sharecropping
A farming system where tenants work land owned by someone else in exchange for a share of the crop. The landowner controlled the accounting, trapping Black farmers in cycles of debt.
Why it matters: Thompson shows how sharecropping created the economic desperation and racial hierarchy that shaped both Till's killers and his family.
White Supremacy
The belief that white people are superior to people of other races, used to justify racial hierarchy, segregation, and violence. Thompson uses this term not to describe only extremists, but the systemic ideology built into law and social life in the Jim Crow South.
Why it matters: Understanding this as a system — not just individual hatred — is central to Thompson's argument.
Double Jeopardy
A legal protection in the U.S. Constitution (5th Amendment) that prevents a person from being tried twice for the same crime. Once acquitted, Bryant and Milam could not be retried for Till's murder — even after they confessed.
Why it matters: This legal protection, designed to prevent government overreach, was exploited to protect murderers. It illustrates how systems built for one purpose can be weaponized.
Primary Source
Firsthand evidence created at or near the time of an event: court transcripts, letters, photographs, eyewitness accounts, official records.
Why it matters: Thompson's credibility as a journalist rests on his primary sources. Identifying them helps you evaluate his claims.
Secondary Source
Materials that interpret or analyze primary sources — history books, documentaries, scholarly articles written after the fact.
Why it matters: Distinguishing primary from secondary sources is a key critical reading skill. It helps you understand how Thompson builds his argument.
Positionality
The way a person's identity — race, gender, class, geography, life experience — shapes how they see, interpret, and are received in the world.
Why it matters: Thompson is explicit about his positionality as a white Mississippian. Recognizing positionality (including your own as a reader) is essential for critical thinking.
The Lost Cause
A revisionist historical myth portraying the Confederacy as noble, the Civil War as being about "states' rights" rather than slavery, and Southern racial violence as justified. Thompson explicitly writes for readers "everywhere the poison of the Lost Cause has spread."
Why it matters: The Lost Cause narrative is one of the mechanisms by which the truth about racial violence was suppressed and reframed.
The Great Migration
The movement of approximately six million Black Americans from the rural South to Northern and Midwestern cities between 1910 and 1970, escaping racial terror and seeking economic opportunity.
Why it matters: Till's family was part of this migration. His Chicago upbringing made him unfamiliar with Mississippi's racial codes — a fact his killers exploited.
Lynching
Extrajudicial killing — murder carried out by a mob or group, often by hanging, outside of any legal process. Used systematically as racial terror against Black Americans in the South. Till's murder is considered a lynching.
Why it matters: Naming Till's murder as a lynching is a political and historical act — it connects it to a pattern of racial terror, not an isolated crime.
Emmett Till Antilynching Act (2022)
Federal legislation signed by President Biden making lynching a federal hate crime. Congress had considered similar bills for over 100 years before passage.
Why it matters: The 100-year delay illustrates how political resistance to acknowledging racial violence has functioned at the highest levels of government.

Key Figures

Emmett Till (1941–1955)
A 14-year-old Black boy from Chicago visiting relatives in the Mississippi Delta. His murder and the acquittal of his killers catalyzed the civil rights movement.
In the book: Thompson works to restore Till's full humanity — not just as a symbol, but as a specific child with a personality, a family, and a life cut short.
Mamie Till-Mobley (1921–2003)
Emmett Till's mother. She insisted on an open-casket funeral and allowed photographs to be published, transforming her son's death into a national awakening. She spent the rest of her life advocating for justice.
In the book: A central moral figure — a woman who converted grief into testimony and action.
