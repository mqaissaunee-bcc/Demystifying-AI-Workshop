When AI Shows Up to Class:
Academic Integrity in the Age of AI

A biology professor's real-world study revealed something surprising: even students sitting in a physical classroom can use AI and other digital tools to earn points without doing the learning. What does that mean for you — and for your education?

Based on a March 2026 Inside Higher Ed article Estimated time: 20–25 minutes Education & AI course
Introduction

Why This Article Matters to You

You probably know that AI tools like ChatGPT exist. You may have used them — or wondered whether other students are using them. A March 2026 article in Inside Higher Ed, a higher-education news site, reports on a study that found AI cheating isn't just a problem in online classes. It's happening in face-to-face classrooms too.

This matters for this course because we're exploring how AI is reshaping teaching and learning. Understanding how and why students use AI to bypass learning — and what educators are trying to do about it — is essential context for any conversation about AI in education.

Key term — Academic Integrity: Doing your own work honestly and following your school's rules about what kinds of help are allowed. When students use AI or other tools to earn credit for work they didn't do, that violates academic integrity.

Article Summary

What the Research Found

A biology professor at Arizona State University (ASU) grew concerned when she noticed that many students in her large in-person class weren't actually showing up — yet were still collecting participation points. They were logging in remotely with digital clickers (devices or apps students use to answer in-class questions) and sometimes even answering for absent classmates.

Curious about how widespread the problem was, she analyzed the structure of 21 biology courses. Her preliminary findings: on average, about 45% of the points in those courses could be earned dishonestly using available digital tools — including AI chatbots that can instantly answer pre-class reading questions.

Students told her researcher team that cheating felt almost unavoidable: exhausted from work or other obligations, knowing that AI could produce an answer in seconds, and worried about falling behind classmates who might already be cheating, many felt they had little choice.

Her conclusion: instructors can no longer assume their classrooms are protected from AI-assisted cheating just because students are physically present. Redesigning how we measure learning — not just adding more detection tools — may be the necessary response.

Key Ideas

Three Ideas to Explore

Idea 1: Being Present Doesn't Mean You're Learning

For years, many instructors have awarded points for participation — showing up, clicking in, answering pre-class questions. The idea was to reward active learning: students engaging with material before and during class rather than just sitting through a lecture.

The ASU research reveals a gap: earning participation credit and actually learning are no longer the same thing. A student can earn a perfect participation score while never setting foot in the room and never genuinely thinking about the material. Digital tools — from clicker workarounds to AI chatbots — make it possible to simulate engagement without real engagement.

Active learning means doing things that help your brain actually process and remember information — asking questions, discussing ideas, solving problems. It's the opposite of passively sitting and listening. Participation points were designed to reward active learning.

Guided Questions — think or write your responses:

  1. What is the difference between performing participation (clicking an answer) and actually participating in learning? Give an example from your own experience.
  2. Why do you think instructors created participation-based grading in the first place? What problem were they trying to solve?
  3. If a student earns full participation credit but doesn't learn anything, who — if anyone — is harmed? Explain your reasoning.

Idea 2: Understanding Why Students Cheat Isn't the Same as Excusing It

The professor in this study was careful to avoid simply blaming students. She pointed out that many students are balancing work, family, and school simultaneously. When it's nearly midnight, an assignment is due, and a chatbot can generate an answer in two seconds, the temptation can feel overwhelming.

She also identified a troubling dynamic: if some students are using AI to complete assignments faster and score higher, honest students may feel pressured to cheat just to stay competitive. This is sometimes called a "race to the bottom" — dishonest behavior spreads because honest behavior seems to put you at a disadvantage.

Important distinction: Understanding why cheating happens — stress, competition, easy access to tools — doesn't mean cheating is acceptable. It means we need to think about the conditions that make cheating more likely, and whether any of those conditions can be changed.

Guided Questions — think or write your responses:

  1. The professor said, "It's easy to blame students." What does she mean, and do you think blaming is the right response? Why or why not?
  2. Have you ever felt pressure to take a shortcut on an assignment because you were exhausted or overwhelmed? (You don't have to share details — just consider: how did that pressure feel?)
  3. How might a community college student's situation — working jobs, caring for family, being the first in their family in college — add to the pressure described in this article?

Idea 3: The Solution Isn't Just "More Detection" — It's Better Design

The professor's main recommendation wasn't to install more AI-detection software or give every student an honor pledge to sign. Instead, she argued that instructors need to redesign how they measure learning — creating assignments and assessments that are harder to fake because they require real, in-the-moment thinking.

Examples she mentioned include in-person, pencil-and-paper exams and physically verifying that students are present and engaged — not just checking a digital log. This is more work for instructors, but she argued it's necessary if participation grades are going to mean anything.

Assessment design refers to how instructors choose to measure whether students have learned. A well-designed assessment makes it hard to earn credit without actually learning — not because it's a "gotcha," but because the task itself requires genuine thinking.

Guided Questions — think or write your responses:

  1. What kinds of assignments are hardest to complete dishonestly using AI? What do those assignments have in common?
  2. The professor suggested that moving to "all high-stakes exams in person" might be one solution. What are the advantages and disadvantages of that approach for students like you?
  3. From what you've learned in this course about AI, what new types of assessments might actually require students to demonstrate real understanding in an AI-present world?
