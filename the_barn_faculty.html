Interactive Common Read Mini-Lesson

The Barn by Wright Thompson

This mini-lesson helps students engage with The Barn: The Secret History of a Murder in Mississippi by Wright Thompson through reading support, reflection, historical context, discussion, and self-check activities.

It is designed for first- and second-year community college students across disciplines and works especially well as a common-read lesson in an LMS.

Quick View

  • Book: The Barn
  • Author: Wright Thompson
  • Course / Discipline: All
  • Use in Course: Common read
  • Audience: First- and second-year students

Approved Learning Outcomes

  1. Summarize the book’s central argument about Emmett Till’s murder and explain how the text connects one crime to larger systems of racism, power, and historical silence.
  2. Identify major themes such as memory, truth, justice, place, inherited history, and community responsibility.
  3. Analyze how Wright Thompson builds meaning through research, storytelling, historical context, and personal reflection.
  4. Evaluate the relationship between individual actions and larger social systems that contributed to injustice.
  5. Make thoughtful connections between the book and current questions about public memory, racial violence, media, education, and civic responsibility.
  6. Use evidence from the text to support interpretation in discussion, writing, and class activities.

Core Overview

The Barn is a nonfiction work that revisits the 1955 murder of Emmett Till in Mississippi. Thompson examines not only the crime itself, but also the land, history, social structures, and silence that surrounded it.

Rather than treating the murder as an isolated event, the book asks readers to examine how communities remember, avoid, reshape, or conceal painful truths.

Why This Book Works as a Common Read

  • It connects one event to larger social and historical systems.
  • It supports discussion across disciplines such as history, English, sociology, criminal justice, education, media studies, and ethics.
  • It models how writers use evidence, narrative, and reflection together.
  • It encourages students to think about truth, responsibility, and public memory.

Historical Context Timeline

Use this timeline to ground your reading in the larger historical story surrounding the book.

1955
Emmett Till, a 14-year-old Black boy from Chicago, is kidnapped and murdered in Mississippi.
1955
Mamie Till-Mobley insists on an open-casket funeral so the public can witness the brutality of racial violence.
1955
An all-white jury acquits Roy Bryant and J. W. Milam, turning the case into a national symbol of injustice.
Later decades
Writers, activists, historians, journalists, and family members continue to revisit the case and its meaning.
Contemporary era
The Barn explores how the legacy of the murder remains connected to land, memory, race, silence, and American identity.

Close Reading & Analysis

  • Track repeated ideas: look for words and passages about land, memory, silence, race, truth, responsibility, and inheritance.
  • Notice author choices: ask how Thompson combines reporting, history, storytelling, and reflection.
  • Study perspective: consider what it means that the author writes from Mississippi and from a position shaped by proximity to the region’s history.
  • Look at structure: notice when the text moves from a single event to broader social meaning.
  • Ask why context matters: identify places where the book insists that one crime cannot be understood by itself.

Reading Support Strategy

  1. Pause after a difficult section and write a one-sentence summary.
  2. Name one emotion or reaction the chapter creates.
  3. Mark one detail or image that seems important.
  4. Ask what larger system or theme the section points toward.

Optional Low-Stakes Writing Prompts

Prompt A: In one paragraph, explain how The Barn turns a specific place into a larger argument about American history.
Prompt B: Choose one theme from the book—memory, justice, silence, place, power, or responsibility—and explain how it develops.
Prompt C: Write a short response explaining what this book suggests about witnesses, communities, and institutions in either protecting truth or hiding it.
Prompt D: Connect the book to a current issue, article, memorial, public controversy, or classroom topic. What similarities or tensions do you notice?

Discussion Questions

  1. Why is it important that the book investigates not only the murder itself but also the world that made it possible?
  2. How does Thompson use place and landscape as part of his argument?
  3. What does the book suggest about the relationship between memory and power?
  4. How does the text challenge simplified versions of history?
  5. What role do institutions such as courts, media, and community norms play in the story?
  6. What responsibilities do writers have when telling traumatic histories?
  7. How can this book function differently in a history course, an English course, a sociology course, or a first-year seminar?
  8. What should readers do with the discomfort or urgency the book creates?
